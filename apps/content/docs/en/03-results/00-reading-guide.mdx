## The Tier System

All findings in this documentation are classified into three tiers:

### Tier 1: Verified (90-100% Confidence)

**Definition**: Findings that are independently verifiable, reproducible, and based on blockchain data or mathematical proof.

**Examples**:
- The Patoshi mining pattern exists (peer-reviewed research)
- The Anna Matrix is 99.59% point-symmetric (mathematically verifiable)
- Block 264 contains a vanity address starting with "1CFB" (blockchain data)

**How to Verify**: Anyone with the data can independently confirm these findings.

### Tier 2: Partial Verification (50-89% Confidence)

**Definition**: Findings with supporting evidence but incomplete verification, or verified facts mixed with interpretation.

**Examples**:
- Collision patterns in the Anna Matrix (verified patterns, interpretation uncertain)
- Mathematical relationships between certain values (calculations correct, significance debatable)
- Historical timeline correlations (dates verified, connection speculative)

**Reader Guidance**: The underlying data is often solid, but conclusions drawn from it may be interpretation.

### Tier 3: Speculation (0-49% Confidence)

**Definition**: Hypotheses, interpretations, and theories without cryptographic or blockchain verification.

**Examples**:
- CFB = Satoshi Nakamoto (5-15% confidence)
- "Hidden messages" in data structures
- Future predictions and "activation events"
- Claims about AGI, neural networks, or "intelligent tissue"

**Reader Guidance**: Treat as interesting hypotheses, NOT as facts. These claims require extraordinary evidence that has not been provided.

## Common Logical Fallacies in This Research

We've identified and attempted to correct these fallacies, but readers should be aware:

### 1. Pattern Matching Fallacy
**Problem**: Finding patterns in data after the fact, then calculating how "unlikely" they are.

**Example**: "The number 27 appears in both X and Y! The probability is astronomical!"

**Reality**: If you look for ANY pattern, you'll find something. This doesn't prove design.

### 2. Multiple Comparisons Problem
**Problem**: Testing many hypotheses without statistical correction.

**Example**: Testing 100 correlations, finding 5 "significant" results, then only reporting those 5.

**Reality**: At p=0.05, you'd expect 5 false positives by chance alone.

### 3. Circular Reasoning
**Problem**: Using the conclusion as part of the evidence.

**Example**: "CFB is Satoshi because Satoshi would use these patterns, and CFB uses these patterns."

**Reality**: This assumes what it's trying to prove.

### 4. Post-Hoc Probability
**Problem**: Calculating the probability of something AFTER it happened.

**Example**: "The probability of THIS EXACT pattern is 1 in 10 million!"

**Reality**: The probability of SOME pattern existing is much higher.
